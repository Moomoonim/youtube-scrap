## 스크립트

Few pages are as iconic as this one. The login form is about 30 years old. Despite improved security on the web, the "password system" still remains vulnerable to cyberattacks. These days, we can do better. Namely with multi-factor authentication, for short: MFA, and Passkeys. Accounts with MFA or Passkeys are at least 120 times better protected against cyberattacks. On top of that: Phishing attacks in Switzerland are currently doubling every year. So it's high time you looked into it. In this video, we'll explain how MFA and Passkeys work, how you can use this technology and what the future of passwords looks like. Digital passwords date back to the early '60s, when a computer still filled a room and access had to be shared among users. Today we know: Passwords by themselves provide not the best protection. Many of us choose passwords that are too simple, and even when they're complex, they only offer basic protection. Fun fact: Just a few months after the invention of the computer password, it was already cracked by a user to be able to use the computer longer. For this reason, MFA was developed in the late '90s, with the aim of securing passwords physically. For example, with a badge. In 2011, the concept was expanded into the form we know today: namely with a temporary code that also has to be entered to access the account. Multi-factor authentication means that you don't get access to your account with just one factor. For example, your password, but you need at least one more, such as possession of a SIM card. Passkeys are the most modern stage of this evolution and were, in 2021, widely introduced. Passkeys work completely without a password and allow you to use only biometric data to access your accounts. Because the technology is still relatively young, it's only supported by a few platforms. Let's take a look at the two technologies in detail. MFA comes in three variants: Message-based, meaning a code via SMS or email, push-based, with a code sent directly to a personal device, and time-based. Time-based MFA is considered the most secure form and works like this: In an authenticator app, you add a code from your account. From this code and the current time, the app calculates a new unique code every 3 seconds. If you now want to log in to your account, you have 30 seconds to look up this code in the app and enter it on the platform. The platform also calculates on its server a code, and if the two match, you're successfully logged in. This system is already common in e-banking and also protects your other accounts extremely well. A big advantage is dispensing with SMS and email. And the time limitation makes the method extremely secure. Passkeys work a bit differently here. A complex key from the platform is securely stored on your own device. When you try to sign in, the platform sends a so-called challenge in the background. You sign this on your device using face recognition or fingerprint with your key and send it back. If the signature is valid, you can access your account. Passkeys work completely passwordless and are therefore even more secure than time-based MFAs. They're also more user-friendly, since the tedious code typing is replaced with a simple scan. The only catch: Because we're in a transition phase to Passkeys, account passwords on most platforms can't be deleted yet. Now that you know how the technologies work, let's set them up together. For MFA, you first need an authenticator app on your smartphone. You can choose apps such as Google and Microsoft Authenticator or 2FAS. Pro tip: Check your password manager to see if it already supports MFA codes. Apps like Apple Passwords and 1Password have this feature built in. Then go to your account's privacy settings and look for the keyword 2FA or MFA. When you enable the option, you get a code that you can enter in the authenticator app and assign to your account. With Passkeys, it's even easier. In your account, look for the keyword Passkey or passwordless login. Now you just need to scan your face or your finger, and then your Passkey is created. Pro tip number 2: Some Passkey providers like Apple Passwords or 1Password even let you securely share Passkeys with your loved ones. That's especially handy for family accounts. So will passwords soon, with these innovations, be a thing of the past? Experts still disagree on that. A complete switch to a technology like Passkeys takes time. And in the meantime, most platforms still rely on classic passwords. That means good password hygiene is and will remain essential. So keep choosing strong, unique passwords, and manage them best with a good password manager.
