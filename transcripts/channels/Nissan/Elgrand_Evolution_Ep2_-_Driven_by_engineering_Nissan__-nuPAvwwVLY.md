I’m Takehito Ichino, Chief Vehicle Engineer for the all-new Elgrand. I’m responsible for every aspect of the vehicle’s performance and driving feel. My role is to bring the whole vehicle together to make sure the targets are being met, and to check that nothing unexpected is likely to happen. In that sense, I carry the overall responsibility. Compared with the previous model, customers’ preferences have changed. We believe the vehicle has shifted from family use toward business use as well. Because business use often means carrying customers in the second-row seats we wanted to place great importance on the second row. Focusing on that, we placed great importance on making the ride quieter and smoother than before in development. First, the powertrain has changed from a regular gasoline engine to the new third-generation e-POWER system. Because e-POWER is essentially electrified, the smooth driving feel of the motor is extremely well-suited to this premium minivan. We made e-4ORCE the only option, with high-torque motors at both the front and rear. Those motors allow this large, heavy minivan to accelerate strongly. The key decision was whether or not to adopt electronically controlled suspension which was a first for this type of minivan. That decision would determine the essence of the vehicle. By using integrated control of e-POWER, e-4ORCE, and the suspension we could create driving performance and drive comfort worthy of the new Elgrand as a flagship. I think that was the biggest decision. Once the concept of the car is decided, we use it as the foundation. From there, we decide how to bring that concept to life. And then, we determine how to incorporate it into the vehicle itself. It comes down to design feasibility; the shapes drawn by the designers. Our job is to make something that can actually be built and mass-produced. Because it is engineering work. When it comes to comfort and control, I’d say the tires play the biggest role. For this Elgrand, we prioritized ride comfort as a premium minivan, so high profile tires were a non-negotiable requirement. In that sense, appearance and comfort involved a bit of a trade-off. We discussed and struggled a lot within the team about achieving freedom of movement. This time we also made the armrest movable. Competitors use fixed armrests but with movable armrests the occupant can move their body more freely. When it comes to long-distance touring, or “grand touring,” they can turn sideways, cross their legs, or sit cross-legged. In the end, we valued the “grand touring” concept and chose real comfort alongside appearance. Even now, I think that was a major decision. At NTC, the Nissan Technical Center, we have a very advanced driving simulator. The simulator moves around dramatically and you can check things with a very realistic driving sensation. We made full use of that and refined the vehicle considerably. We do, of course, drive on test tracks to verify performance. Of course, I confirmed it myself on the test course and on public roads. I was confident, but the moment I felt it most strongly was when external people, including journalists, drove the vehicle and gave us positive comments. That was when I felt that we had created the car I had wanted to create. This new Elgrand is something we have really poured our heart and soul into. More than just a minivan, it is a vehicle packed with so many things, capable of doing so much. And the quiet, smooth ride is truly wonderful. I truly look forward to the production vehicle being manufactured in a way that firmly maintains the quality expected of a premium minivan.
