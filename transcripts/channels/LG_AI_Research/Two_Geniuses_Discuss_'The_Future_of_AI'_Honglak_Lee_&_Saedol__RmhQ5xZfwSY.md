## 스크립트

Maybe this could actually elevate human thinking itself… This is how we're going to change the world! Today’s young generation in their 20s is feeling deeply confused. AI isn't perfect yet... Just as if we’re brainstorming… Sedol Lee UNIST, Collaborating Professor Board Game Author Former Professional Baduk (Go) Player AlphaGo Match – The Last Human to Defeat an AI Honglak Lee LG AI Research, co-Head, CSAI University of Michigan, Computer Science, Professor Former Google Brain, Research Scientist Selected as one of the Top 10 AI Researchers by IEEE AI Scientist &amp; AI Victor Two geniuses talk about the future of AI Episode 1: Discussing Korea’s AI Did you have any connection with LG in the past? Yes, I believe I was around 15 at the time. Back then, I was a young talent supported by LG. And during the 2016 AlphaGo match, I remember wearing a smart watch and this dress shirt with the logo on it when I played. How did you watch the AlphaGo match? I followed it very closely. I truly hoped you would win on behalf of all humans. But at the same time, since Google had taken on such a major challenge, I also felt there must have been a solid reason behind it. Nine years since AlphaGo shook the world. After that historic match… I actually retired at the end of 2019. That’s just three-and-a-few months after AlphaGo appeared. I had played Go for 30 years, and yet everything felt meaningless in an instant. It was frightening. In a way, the Go world simply experienced this shift the fastest— or maybe I was the first to face it. But the same thing can happen to any industry today. If I speak from a positive perspective, I don’t regret the decision to retire in 2019. Times had changed, and instead of clinging to the past, I felt we should move forward by working with AI. There’s no need to fear it. So how are things now? Since AlphaGo, how has AI technology continued to develop? I believe AlphaGo showed the world the tremendous potential of AI, and in many ways, it was one of the most monumental moments in the field. Whenever new AI algorithms emerge, there’s always this excitement “Ah, with this, we’re going to change the world.” Researchers and industry teams dive in, applying these methods and building new technologies. But historically, each time a promising algorithm appeared, there came a point where we hit a wall. Progress would surge, then plateau that pattern has always existed. However, with transformer-based models like GPT, we’re seeing something very different. Even if we pour in endless amounts of data, the performance keeps improving without saturation. And since nearly all the data we have can be converted into what we call “tokens,” we can feed them directly into a transformer model and end up with extremely powerful systems. The transformer architecture itself has remarkable strengths. And what we call the “scaling law” is still advancing without any clear limit. That’s why many believe this trajectory could ultimately lead us to AGI and even ASI. For these reasons, big tech companies and governments around the world continue to invest heavily in this area. - Global AI Power Struggle Intensifies - Transformers Ignite the Era of Infinite AI Competition - AI Supremacy Race: A Battle for Survival - As AI Performance Soars Without Limit, Who Will Lead? From the center of the AI transformation, a look at Korea’s present and future. If we take the U.S. as a level 10, where would you say Korea stands? I’d say we’re around a 7. 7? The U.S. is a 10, and you’d still say 7? Yes. That sounds a bit high… If you look beyond the U.S. and China, there aren’t many countries producing truly strong AI models. So in that sense, Korea isn’t actually that far behind. In terms of GPU capacity or overall scale, you could argue we’re smaller— and that’s true. But the U.S. excels at generating bold, creative ideas, and then heavily funding the ones that show promise. That process requires an astronomical amount of money. China, on the other hand, works with far fewer resources. They minimize trial and error and follow the path efficiently. So the risk of failure is lower for them? Exactly. So with even fewer resources, we can follow as well. I think we’ve been doing a good job keeping up. Our biggest bottleneck has always been GPUs— computing resources in general. But now, through the national foundation model project that includes LG, we’re receiving government support. Over the next two to three years, I believe this will fill a lot of our gaps and create real synergy. China is also a country we need to keep watching closely. Especially in the area of physical AI. Physical AI: AI whose decision-making capabilities are integrated with robots, sensors, and other physical devices, enabling it to act directly in the real world. Since Korea is a strong manufacturing nation, I’m worried that falling behind in industrial robots and the broader field of physical AI could make things very difficult for us. What are your thoughts on this? China is doing very well in the robotics hardware space especially in producing robots at low cost, training them effectively, and creating robots with basic intelligence. For Korea, I believe physical AI will be an important mid- to long-term direction. The question is how we can develop smarter AI systems, integrate them well with robots, and apply them effectively to manufacturing and other industries. These are areas we need to continue developing over the long run. This is actually a very sensitive question. Korea has so many regulations what do you think absolutely needs to change? Well, I don’t personally feel that regulation is preventing us from doing things or creating any major bottleneck. Among various government initiatives recently, I think the increased support for GPUs which have long been a bottleneck is very positive. So from a research perspective, regulations aren’t really a major obstacle, is that what you’re saying? Yes. Korea already has a solid pool of AI talent and is doing well in data as well. But we’ve been significantly lacking in computing power. If we build this infrastructure like laying down a national highway it could serve as a powerful catalyst not only for industry but also for academia and the broader research community. Given how essential GPUs are for today’s AI market, even though they haven’t fully rolled out yet, the government’s announcement is meaningful. Korea really needs to build a strong AI ecosystem. If we don’t develop our own large-scale models, we’ll have no choice but to rely on external models like ChatGPT in the U.S. or other big-tech APIs. That means sending all our data to them, and we would also lose the opportunity to accumulate our own data and build high-quality models internally. Korean NPU companies and startups are performing extremely well, and if we nurture them properly, they could play a major role in building Korea’s AI ecosystem. Achieving commercial success and creating a virtuous cycle will be crucial moving forward. Korea selects five national teams to build its own “Sovereign AI” Full-scale launch of the “AI Top 3 Nations” project How do you think AI will enrich our lives? How will these changes reshape our lives in the years ahead? How do you think AI will enrich our lives? To begin with, it will greatly enhance productivity for both individuals and organizations. But beyond productivity, I believe AI may bring major innovation in solving humanity’s long-standing conundrums such as disease, health, and human longevity. If AI enables us to resolve scientific problems that humanity has long struggled with, I believe the benefits could be significant. Even Demis Hassabis, who created AlphaGo, later developed AlphaFold and ultimately received a Nobel Prize for that achievement. In Go, there is a familiar question: If I were to play a professional player from 100 or 200 years ago, who would win? I have said that I would probably win. Not because my reasoning ability is inherently superior, but because technological progress has steadily advanced, and I have learned from that accumulated development. But that does not mean I would win because I think better than players of the past. That is why I wonder whether, by collaborating with AI, human reasoning itself may rise as well. In Go, that is exactly what people are hoping for now. As players work with AI, the level of play has advanced to the point where you sometimes ask, “Is this really a human move?” The contrast is that stark. Compared with the way games were played before, it feels as though the underlying reasoning has shifted to an entirely different level. And just as Go is moving in that direction, I think that through collaboration with AI, the overall level of human thinking may also increase. I feel the same way. In the past, when AI didn’t exist, people produced results relying solely on their own knowledge, skills, and professional abilities. But now, I think we need to think more about how we can collaborate effectively with AI. For example, since there are so many remarkable AI-driven tools available, it might be good to take on more challenging tasks— such as using them without limitation to produce the best possible outcome. In the end, I think it comes down to this: as we work with AI in areas where we each excel, things will naturally evolve in that direction.
