## 스크립트

We brought Atlas to the New York, New Jersey Stadium for one of the round of 16 games and we were able to use the robot to hand the game ball off to the referees at halftime to start the second half of the game. It was great. I'm Seth Davis. I am a senior program manager on the Atlas team. &gt;&gt; [music] &gt;&gt; It was really important with Atlas to build a robot that could kind of do anything. That we could do manufacturing jobs, that we could do warehouse jobs and the fact that we can take that robot and use it to do soccer moves, to be part of a show like this is really exciting. &gt;&gt; Kevin, are we filming? &gt;&gt; We have a team that can pivot both from like getting really deep into the technical details of how to make a robot walk, how to make a robot pick something up and manipulate it really accurately to making the robot do things like kicking a soccer ball, carrying a soccer ball, interacting with people at these kinds of events in a way that isn't just precise in an engineering way, but also kind of makes you feel something about what that robot is capable of and what that robot means to the future. The walking robot is the exciting and really visible part of what we're doing, but that's not really what we're delivering when a customer buys a robot. When the case of Spot, what they want is not a robot, but they want inspections. They want to understand the health of their plant and the robot is just a way to do that and that's becoming more and more important with Atlas as we go from the prototype stage where we're just making the robot do cool things like backflips and useful things like picking up parts to being an actual useful member of a factory team that can do the jobs that are necessary to make a factory function. The robot is really just one part of a larger system and at Boston Dynamics we've gotten really good at building that whole system around the robot.
