## 스크립트

It's incredibly easy to fall into&nbsp; the trap of using AI for everything.&nbsp;&nbsp; Many teams are starting to integrate AI agents&nbsp; all across their systems. And why wouldn't they?&nbsp;&nbsp; AI agent systems are undeniably powerful. There's&nbsp; no doubt about it. But we need to stay strategic&nbsp;&nbsp; about where and whether we want to inject AI&nbsp; into our systems. AI is not magic and it's not&nbsp;&nbsp; the best solution for everything. Just like&nbsp; any engineering decision, choosing to use AI&nbsp;&nbsp; or not and at what scale involves trade-offs&nbsp; across accuracy, cost, complexity, and risk.&nbsp;&nbsp; Most problems in software and data systems can&nbsp; be solved in one of these four ways. Humans&nbsp;&nbsp; for humanity and judgment. Rules or codebased&nbsp; solution for deterministic logic. machine learning&nbsp;&nbsp; for statistical pattern recognition and generative&nbsp; AI systems for flexible reasoning and generation.&nbsp;&nbsp; These aren't some ladder that you progress through&nbsp; from human to agents. In fact, they are distinct&nbsp;&nbsp; options each with a very specific role. The goal&nbsp; is to pick the right system for the right problem.&nbsp;&nbsp; Let's start with humans. I may have a little&nbsp; bias here, but humans are great at making complex&nbsp;&nbsp; judgments. People can handle ambiguity and&nbsp; edge cases very well, making complex decisions&nbsp;&nbsp; with incomplete or conflicting information.&nbsp; Particularly, they can also apply context,&nbsp;&nbsp; ethics, and accountability in ways that machines&nbsp; cannot. Humans should drive tasks when there are&nbsp;&nbsp; high stakes decisions, situations&nbsp; requiring ownership or liability,&nbsp;&nbsp; and cases of significant ambiguity. Some examples&nbsp; of these are hiring decisions, medical diagnosis,&nbsp;&nbsp; legal interpretation, and large-scale strategy&nbsp; or financial decisions. If something goes wrong,&nbsp;&nbsp; accountability must be with a real person. Now&nbsp; for the trade-offs. Human judgment is high quality&nbsp;&nbsp; but expensive, slow, and very hard to scale. If&nbsp; you think about hiring a candidate for a job,&nbsp;&nbsp; you could screen resumes automatically, but&nbsp; the final decision involves more nuance such&nbsp;&nbsp; as team fit and communication skills, which&nbsp; are not something you want fully automated.&nbsp;&nbsp; On the other hand, you would never rely on humans&nbsp; to manually review every single transaction on a&nbsp;&nbsp; credit card network. That's millions per second.&nbsp; So let's look into some of those cases where a&nbsp;&nbsp; code or rules-based deterministic system might be&nbsp; beneficial. In particular, a traditional software&nbsp;&nbsp; solution is good at executing clear, stable logic&nbsp; and producing consistent exact outputs at scale.&nbsp;&nbsp; Rule-based systems are ideal to use for&nbsp; tasks when logic is known and explicit.&nbsp;&nbsp; For example, if X do Y. Requirements&nbsp; are stable and errors are unacceptable.&nbsp;&nbsp; Some examples of these are payment processing,&nbsp; input validation, data transformations, and most&nbsp;&nbsp; importantly, security and access control. Code&nbsp; is fast, cheap, reliable, and very interpretable.&nbsp;&nbsp; It's generally straightforward to test, debug, and&nbsp; scale. If I want to process a credit card payment,&nbsp;&nbsp; for example, I want a code system to check if&nbsp; balance is sufficient and approve or decline.&nbsp;&nbsp; This should be as fast as possible&nbsp; deterministic and have no room for error.&nbsp;&nbsp; Using AI in this case would introduce unnecessary&nbsp; risk. Same for validating an email address format.&nbsp;&nbsp; You don't need a model. You need a simple rule.&nbsp; But where fail the rules fail and begin to break&nbsp;&nbsp; down is for cases where conditions are constantly&nbsp; changing and logic becomes just too complex.&nbsp;&nbsp; Take fraud detection for example. A static&nbsp; rule like flag any transactions over $1,000&nbsp;&nbsp; quickly becomes useless against fraudulent&nbsp; attackers whose behaviors evolve. That's where&nbsp;&nbsp; machine learning and statistics come in. Machine&nbsp; learning excels at learning patterns in structured&nbsp;&nbsp; data and making probabilistic predictions. Machine&nbsp; learning is ideal to use when patterns exist&nbsp;&nbsp; but aren't very obvious, rules are too complex&nbsp; to define manually and when you need predictions.&nbsp;&nbsp; Some common areas of use are fraud detection,&nbsp; customer churn prediction, demand forecasting,&nbsp;&nbsp; and recommendation systems. Here's an example&nbsp; in practice. If I want a system that forecasts&nbsp;&nbsp; product demand, a human might rely on intuition&nbsp; and bias and a rule or code solutions may be&nbsp;&nbsp; too simplistic to pick up on the nuances of demand&nbsp; trends. This is where machine learning shines.&nbsp;&nbsp; Able to learn complex trends and patterns in&nbsp; data, resulting in more accurate predictions&nbsp;&nbsp; and improved model outputs, making it the&nbsp; ideal system for this particular task.&nbsp;&nbsp; Machine learning models scale very well&nbsp; and are generally predictable. However,&nbsp;&nbsp; they do require more monitoring and ongoing&nbsp; maintenance as over time there can be model&nbsp;&nbsp; drift and low flexibility outside the initially&nbsp; defined problem scope. Machine learning models&nbsp;&nbsp; do also have varying degrees of explanability, but&nbsp; if you needed to write a report on what it thinks&nbsp;&nbsp; the societal causes of these demand trends are,&nbsp; you're out of luck. While the task of analyzing&nbsp;&nbsp; and predicting trends over massive structured data&nbsp; may not be ideal for your typical LLM, they are&nbsp;&nbsp; good at working with unstructured data and tasks&nbsp; that involve reasoning, synthesis, and language.&nbsp;&nbsp; Generative AI systems like LLMs and agents&nbsp; are best to use when inputs are unstructured&nbsp;&nbsp; like text, documents, and other media, tasks&nbsp; require interpretation or transformation.&nbsp;&nbsp; Flexibility matters more than precision and&nbsp; some amount of error is tolerated. Some common&nbsp;&nbsp; examples are retrieval, augmented generation&nbsp; for question answering over document sets,&nbsp;&nbsp; large document summarization, code generation, and&nbsp; multi-step workflows with agents. Take a customer&nbsp;&nbsp; support system for example. Instead of hard-coded&nbsp; responses to user questions, an AI agent can&nbsp;&nbsp; understand intent, pull relevant knowledge,&nbsp; and generate a unique and helpful reply.&nbsp;&nbsp; Trying to do so with rules alone would be nearly&nbsp; impossible. Generative AI systems are incredibly&nbsp;&nbsp; flexible and time to success is very fast with&nbsp; LLMs being such good generalists at tasks.&nbsp;&nbsp; However, the tradeoff is non-determinism&nbsp; in answers leads to difficulty in testing,&nbsp;&nbsp; correctness not being guaranteed across runs, and&nbsp; they tend to be significantly more costly at scale&nbsp;&nbsp; than these other tech solutions. That is really&nbsp; the key to knowing whether or not to build agents&nbsp;&nbsp; and LLMs for your tasks. How much non-determinism&nbsp; and uncertainty are you willing to tolerate?&nbsp;&nbsp; For example, if I want an AI agent&nbsp; to analyze my spending for the year,&nbsp;&nbsp; I can't just feed in thousands of transactions and&nbsp; expect it to create a non-h hallucinated answer&nbsp;&nbsp; or even just get the calculations correct.&nbsp; Instead, I might want a calculator tool that&nbsp;&nbsp; uses code and math for determinism and traditional&nbsp; machine learning to identify trends over time. And of course, I might want an LLM to still&nbsp; generate a nice, clean natural language report&nbsp;&nbsp; at the end. Well, these hybrid systems tend to be&nbsp; the most successful using all of these types of&nbsp;&nbsp; solutions in conjunction properly balancing the&nbsp; correct tool for the task. When someone says to&nbsp;&nbsp; make everything agents, this is not literal.&nbsp; All of the best agents and modern solutions&nbsp;&nbsp; use some combination of all of these types of&nbsp; systems. It's important to identify and think&nbsp;&nbsp; out where each of these are most appropriate to&nbsp; apply. Here's an actionable heruristic. Use humans&nbsp;&nbsp; when you need judgment, accountability,&nbsp; and ethics or have high-risk workflows.&nbsp;&nbsp; Use rules or traditional code when you need&nbsp; clearly defined logic as if, if X happens,&nbsp;&nbsp; do Y. And that logic won't change&nbsp; often. Use traditional machine learning&nbsp;&nbsp; when you want to find patterns and&nbsp; predictions in past data. Use generative AI when you need to interpret, generate, or reason&nbsp; over complex inputs. And flexibility matters more&nbsp;&nbsp; than precision. In general, what's most important&nbsp; is matching the technology to your use case.&nbsp;&nbsp; and choosing the right kind of intelligence for&nbsp; your tasks. Most failures to get to production&nbsp;&nbsp; aren't going to come from bad models, but rather&nbsp; from choosing the wrong system in the first place.&nbsp;&nbsp; The future of technology isn't about replacing&nbsp; humans or putting AI everywhere. Great systems&nbsp;&nbsp; are built by making disciplined choices about how&nbsp; decisions get made across humans, rules, models,&nbsp;&nbsp; and machines. The most successful AI systems&nbsp; likely won't be the ones using the most AI.&nbsp;&nbsp; It will be the ones who consistently choose&nbsp; the right kind of intelligence for the problem.&nbsp;&nbsp; Because the most important AI skill&nbsp; is knowing when not to use it.
