For years, the default for LLM inference was BF16. High quality but heavy. Every weight, every activation stored at a relatively high precision. Then the industry moved to FP8. half the memory, higher throughput, and for most models, quality barely moved. NVFP4 pushes this further with 4bit floatingpoint weights on Nvidia GPUs. I know 4-bit sounds aggressive, but with NVFP4 quantized models, you can get performance speedups while staying close to the original models accuracy. This isn't just round everything to four bits and hope it works, though. NVFP4 uses an FP4 format called E2M1. One sign bit, two exponent bits, and one Mantisa bit. Many FP4 formats share that layout, but they often rely on a single shared scale across a block of values. NVFP4 goes further. Instead of one scale per block, it uses fine grain block scaling, one FP8 scale for every 16 values. Locally, within small neighborhoods of weights, you keep precise relative representations. On top of that, there's a second FP32 scale for the whole tensor, so you preserve the global distribution, too. Two levels of scaling instead of one. local precision where the data needs it and global range so you don't lose the overall structure of the model. That's why quality holds up better than you'd expect at this precision. The practical result, if your deployment is weight memory bound, you may fit a model on fewer GPUs, increase bat size, or serve more traffic on the same hardware.
