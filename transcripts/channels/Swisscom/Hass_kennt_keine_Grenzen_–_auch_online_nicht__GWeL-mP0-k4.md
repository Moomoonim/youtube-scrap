## 스크립트

Wow, it’s hard to believe what’s going on here! For example, this says: “Are you in it for the football or all you can f***?” under a post by Alisha Lehmann. Really, some people… It’s as if they don’t understand that they’re talking about a real person. Besides a lot of blatant sexism, the players also get racist comments. Many players’ Insta pages are now moderated, but on TikTok it feels like people can write whatever they want. Surely there’s something that can be done about it! That’s what we’re talking about today: hate speech and how not to be just a passive bystander. There’s a good chance that you’ve already encountered hate speech. More than half the Swiss population comes across hate speech at least once a month. Hate speech is when a person or a group is insulted, mocked or discriminated against on the basis of certain characteristics such as age, origin, sexual orientation or gender. Incredibly, the majority of hate comments online are posted by a small group of people. Most are politically motivated males between the ages of 20 and 40. As much as 70% of hate comments are posted by just 1% of users. Unbelievable! They like social media because they’re more or less anonymous and there’s nothing to stop you spreading hate. For the recipients of hate speech, comments like this can have a serious impact on their emotional and mental health. Actually it is really easy to take a stand against this little hate group, by practising what’s called ‘counter-speech’. This basically means contradicting hateful comments. It’s a way of pointing out to the haters that their words can be hurtful – because they obviously don’t realise it themselves. In other words, you simply reply to the hateful comment. Like this, for example: “Try putting yourself in the position of the person on your screen before you post messages like that.” According to a study by ETH, this can lead to haters thinking about their actions and spreading less hate in future. And counter-speech also has a positive effect on the silent audience. They give hate comments fewer likes and interact less with them. Of course, we decided to try using the Swisscom account to reply to hate comments. The reactions were varied. Either they didn’t respond at all or they shot back with more hate. However, there were also users and even players who supported our comments with a written post or a like, and some haters deleted their hateful comments themselves after our counter-speech. Vanished into thin air. I am still shocked that some people spread so much hate, and am trying to grasp what’s behind it. I’m being helped by Sophie Achermann from the Stop Hate Speech project. Why do people do this? Unfortunately, we don’t know what drives these people. But it is clear that some of them really are extremely full of hate. I find it very hard to believe that they’re otherwise able to lead a contented, happy life. But back to the victims of hate speech, because they’re who we’re talking about. Sophie gives me tips on how to support them. I think it’s very important simply to react. If you don’t know how counter-speech works, it can also be very helpful to write to the victim. Maybe send them a DM. Because when you experience hate speech, you feel very isolated and alone and also very hurt. Showing solidarity and standing up for someone in this position can make a big difference to the person. She goes on to say that you can always report hateful comments and, for very serious insults, there are organisations that can support you in pressing charges. The conversation with Sophie showed me that there are already a lot of people who are taking a stand against hate speech. And yet: one in ten Swiss internet users has been insulted or threatened online at least once. What can you do if you find yourself the target of hate speech? We don’t usually recommend that the victims of hate speech engage in counter-speech themselves, because dealing with the hate speech and then responding to it is, in our opinion, asking too much. We recommend that people who are frequently exposed to hate speech gather a group of people around them, who can take care of things on their behalf and practise counter-speech, and perhaps even have access to the account involved so they can delete the offensive content. That’s what a lot of female footballers do on Instagram. Because you really don’t have to put up with the hate. What I’ve learned from this research: It takes courage to speak up against hate, but doing so can make a difference. The next time I encounter hate on the internet, I will post a comment or say something nice to the person concerned. And what will you do?
