Part 2: AI-Driven Future As someone who teaches at a university, I find that people in their twenties today are, in many ways, deeply confused. I still remember when smartphones first emerged and the online era truly began— back then, people in their twenties were genuinely enthusiastic and energized by those changes. But with the arrival of the AI era, it feels different. From early to late twenties, many seem unusually uncertain and unsettled. Given this situation, how do you think they should move forward? I sometimes think that today’s university students are almost a native generation when it comes to using tools like ChatGPT. At the same time, however, I do have some concerns. By relying heavily on generative AI tools, they may be engaging less deeply in the process of learning itself— thinking and struggling through problems on their own a bit less than before. When we were students, we didn’t have access to sophisticated AI tools, so we had to do almost everything ourselves. If we were given a difficult assignment, we might spend days—sometimes even nights— wrestling with it, struggling to find answers. Through that process, we practiced expressing our own thoughts and ideas in our own words. Admittedly, it wasn’t very efficient. But precisely because it was inefficient, it became a form of training. That process, in hindsight, helped shape how we think. For the older generation, the knowledge and experience accumulated over many years (Previous Generation, Individuality, Opportunity, Knowledge, Thinking, Style, Experience, Attempt – AI) now serve as a foundation that can be effectively combined with AI. In contrast, for those in their early twenties or even early thirties, there simply hasn’t been enough time to build up that foundation yet. As a result, it can actually be more challenging for them to meaningfully integrate AI into their work or thinking. That’s the impression I get. This may be a slightly different topic, but to be honest, I’m really not good at drawing. Neither am I. But now, with AI, we’re able to create images that we once could only imagine. Things I used to think, “I’ll never be able to do this in my lifetime,” suddenly feel possible in the age of AI. In that sense, the range of things I can do has expanded tremendously. As this range expands, do you think it could actually widen the gap between people instead? I do think that the gap can widen depending on how effectively people use these tools. That’s why education around AI is so important— learning how to use AI well, how to integrate it, and how to combine it with other ideas to create greater value. Those kinds of skills and training will be essential. Since AI can now handle many of the basics, the scope of what individuals are capable of has indeed expanded significantly. At the same time, the importance of thinking itself seems to have grown even more than before. When we talk about the kind of talent we value today—or in the future— we’re not looking for people who simply write down the answers AI provides. Rather, true talent lies in those who can collaborate with AI, leverage AI tools, and generate far better outcomes as a result. So in the end, starting now, learning how to work with AI— and perhaps building communities around that— may be one of the most important things. Spaces where people can exchange ideas and move forward together. Yes, having such communities would be incredibly helpful. Whether within universities or through other forms of communities, creating those environments where people can grow together may become the real source of strength in navigating this transition. Yes, I think that’s a very good point. 92% of Korean university students use AI when working on assignments “ChatGPT May Be Eroding Critical Thinking Skills,” MIT AI should move beyond ‘bans’ toward frameworks for creative use Personally, I believe that the most important skills in the age of AI are the ability to ask creative questions, to make independent judgments, and to communicate based on those judgments. When I hear people say they feel stuck, it’s often because they don’t really understand what it means to ask a creative question. They may be able to interpret an answer and make a certain level of independent judgment once they receive it— but the very first step, asking a creative question, feels unclear to them. They ask, “What does that even mean?” In a way, it helps to think of AI as a brainstorming partner. In English, people often use the phrase “What if.” From a brainstorming perspective, rather than trying to come up with a perfect question from the very beginning, it’s better to start with conversation. As you talk naturally, ideas begin to emerge. At first, those ideas may be very rough, but as you refine them, they can develop into something quite strong. Ultimately, I think it’s important to actually try making something with AI. In my case, I teach a class where students design and produce their own board games. In the past, creating a board game required humans to do everything themselves. Today, that’s no longer the case. The process now involves asking questions to AI, receiving responses, and then making judgments about them— that middle layer, so to speak. And it doesn’t have to be a board game. The same applies if, for example, you’re creating a short book. Of course, the initial idea still has to come from a human but in the middle of the process, you can ask AI questions, evaluate its responses, select what works, and gradually shape the final outcome. Collaborating with AI to generate something—creating something that didn’t exist before— is an extremely meaningful practice. I feel very much the same way. Accumulating experiences where you actually create something through AI can be tremendously helpful. AI is not perfect—at least, not yet. That’s why the ability to evaluate AI’s outputs is so critical. What truly matters is having the judgment to assess those results: thinking about how they can be improved, applying critical thinking rather than accepting them at face value, challenging them when necessary, and correcting what is wrong. I believe these feedback skills—questioning, refining, and pushing AI’s outputs further— will become increasingly important going forward. This is an era in which collaboration with AI— rather than competition—is what truly matters. And as a result, critical thinking becomes even more important. Within this broader context, what direction is LG AI Research taking? In fact, this may be the most essential question of all: where does LG’s AI stand today, and how is it evolving? At LG AI Research, our ultimate goal—our ideal vision— is to build truly excellent 'Expert AI’. Our aim is to create AI that significantly enhances the productivity of experts, helps drive breakthroughs, and opens up new horizons. A good example would be forecasting AI. Forecasting AI can be used to predict product demand, raw material prices, or particularly in the energy solutions domain—lithium prices. If we can accurately predict lithium prices in advance, companies, like LG Energy Solution, we can make better decisions about when to purchase materials and how to schedule production. Similarly, if we can accurately forecast financial products or stock prices, we can leverage that capability to build financial agents. Ultimately, agents that excel at prediction can play a major role in supporting business decision-making. That sounds quite remarkable. Yes, that’s right. Of course, this will continue to evolve but at the current stage, what level have you reached— and to what level do you aim to advance, and by when? We are pursuing two main directions. First, in order to build expert AI, we are working closely with LG affiliates across a wide range of domains, using AI to solve specialized tasks and generate tangible business outcomes. At the same time, we are driving AI transformation across the entire LG Group— moving toward a future where AI takes on a much greater role in everyday business operations through expert AI systems. Second, to do all of this effectively a certain level of fundamentally intelligent AI is required. So there needs to be a strong core. Exactly. From a model perspective, the EXAONE model developed by LG AI Research is recognized as one of the top-tier AI models— not only in Korea but also globally, ranking around the top ten worldwide. We are focused on building AI models of the highest caliber that can serve as the foundation for expert AI systems. In addition to developing the EXAONE model itself, we are also working on a wide range of initiatives to ensure that the AI we build can be effectively applied across the diverse businesses of LG affiliates.
